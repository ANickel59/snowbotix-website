Obstacle Detection & Geofencing

Defined boundaries. Active sensing. Controlled response.

Snowbotix uses two overlapping controls: geofencing keeps the robot inside defined operating zones, and 360-degree obstacle detection identifies people, vehicles, objects, and site hazards in the area around the unit. When either detects a condition that crosses a threshold, the system stops, logs the event, and escalates for human review.

01 — The Two Control Layers

Geofencing defines where. Sensing controls what happens inside.

Geofencing layer

Defines the operating boundary before work begins

Operating area boundaries

Define the outer perimeter of the robot's work zone during the mapping and approval process.

Exclusion zones

Mark specific areas the robot must not enter: loading areas, emergency access, restricted surfaces, sensitive equipment.

Time-based zone rules

Some zones can be active during off-peak windows and excluded during peak-traffic hours.

Pile zone mapping

Snow and material movement is directed to designated collection areas that are part of the approved site map.

Sensitive surface flags

Areas with specific surface constraints — pavers, exposed aggregate, specialty coatings — can be marked for adjusted behavior or exclusion.

Obstacle detection layer

Identifies conditions in the area around the unit during operation

360-degree sensing perimeter

Sensors maintain coverage around the unit during autonomous operation, not just ahead of travel direction.

Common detection conditions

People and pedestrians, vehicles and equipment, fixed objects, dropped material, site debris, construction items, and unexpected surface hazards.

Six-foot sensing radius

The sensor perimeter is designed to detect activity before the unit reaches the condition, providing response time for controlled stopping.

Automatic halt behavior

When a detection threshold is crossed, the unit stops, logs the event, and holds position. It does not continue past an obstruction.

Escalation path

Logged events are visible to the remote supervisor for review, rerouting, manual operation, or facility escalation.

03 — Stop-Log-Escalate Framework

Every detection event follows a defined response path.

When obstacle detection or geofence logic triggers, the response is not discretionary. The system stops. It logs. It escalates. Human judgment re-enters the operating model at that point.

04 — Site Environment Applications

Geofencing and sensing requirements vary by site type.

Healthcare campuses

Emergency routes, ambulance access, and helipads mapped as exclusion or priority zones. Pedestrian paths monitored during peak care hours. Supervisor alerts for any stop event near clinical areas.

Airports

Airside boundaries, sterile zones, and secured access areas require carefully scoped geofences reviewed with facilities and security teams. Routes operate within defined service boundaries.

Logistics & intermodal yards

Active truck traffic, docks, and shifting equipment require line-of-sight manual operation in high-vehicle zones. Geofencing separates pedestrian corridors from vehicle lanes.

Municipalities

Sidewalks, park paths, and transit stops require defined operating boundaries and public-facing sensing. Stop events and boundary incidents are logged for municipal review.

Defense & government

Restricted area exclusions, perimeter mapping, and zone-level operating rules reviewed with facility security. All events logged for command review.

Commercial contractors

Multi-site deployments require each property's boundaries and exclusions mapped separately. Stop-event records support customer-facing service reporting.

FAQ

Questions about obstacle detection and geofencing.

What does geofencing actually prevent?

Geofencing prevents the robot from operating outside its defined zone. It is set during site mapping and reviewed before deployment. The robot will not cross into an excluded area during autonomous operation — it stops at the defined boundary.

What happens if someone walks into the route?

If a person enters the sensing perimeter during autonomous operation, the unit halts. The event is logged with timestamp, location, and sensor data, and escalated to the remote supervisor. The robot does not resume until the area is clear and the event is reviewed.

Can the robot navigate around an obstacle on its own?

The current operating model does not include autonomous rerouting around obstacles. When a detection event occurs, the unit stops and holds. The remote supervisor reviews the event and determines whether to resume, reroute, or switch to manual operation.

How are exclusion zones defined?

Exclusion zones are mapped during site assessment and reviewed by the facility team before deployment. They can cover specific areas such as emergency access routes, loading zones, restricted surfaces, mechanical equipment, and sensitive material storage.

What is the sensing radius around the unit?

The sensing perimeter is designed to detect people, vehicles, and objects at approximately six feet around the unit during autonomous operation. This provides response time for a controlled stop before the unit reaches the condition.

Are stop events recorded?

Yes. Every stop event is logged with location, timestamp, sensor data, and where configured, a photo. These records are part of the service operating record available for supervisor review.

Can geofence zones be updated after deployment?

Yes. Zone updates go through a review process. Boundaries, exclusions, and time-based rules can be adjusted based on facility changes, seasonal access patterns, or operational feedback.

Does detection work in snow and poor visibility conditions?

Sensor performance in adverse weather conditions is addressed in the site assessment and is part of the operating threshold review. Deployment in specific weather conditions should be confirmed during the site evaluation process.
