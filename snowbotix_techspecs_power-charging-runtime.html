Technology Specifications / Power, Charging & Runtime

Electric power for recurring outdoor maintenance routes.

Snowbotix operates on an approximately 7 kW lithium iron phosphate battery with a 4–6 hour typical runtime, approximately 1-minute hot-swap capability, wireless dock charging on standard 110V power, and up to 60 days standby. Mission planning tools in the fleet portal track battery state in real time and trigger auto-return before runtime runs out.

01 — Core Power Specifications

LFP platform built for outdoor commercial work cycles.

Lithium iron phosphate chemistry was chosen for its thermal stability, discharge characteristics in cold temperatures, and long cycle life relative to other lithium chemistries. These properties matter for recurring outdoor maintenance work where the battery regularly operates at or below freezing.

Battery capacity

~7 kW

LFP (lithium iron phosphate) — stable discharge in cold temperatures, long cycle life

Typical runtime

4–6 hr

Per charge cycle. Depends on work mode, attachment, conditions, and route. See runtime factors.

Hot-swap time

~1 min

At geotagged swap station. Enables continuous multi-shift operations without long charging pauses.

Standby duration

60 days

Up to 60 days standby capability — supports seasonal gaps, storm-staging, and low-frequency deployment models.

These are estimated ranges for mission planning. Actual runtime varies based on site and operating conditions. Mission planning in the fleet portal uses real-time battery state to manage dispatch timing and trigger auto-return before the battery hits the low-battery threshold.

04 — Runtime Factors

What affects how long the robot runs per charge.

Runtime is not a fixed number. Every outdoor maintenance route has a different power draw profile. Plan around ranges, not single-point estimates, and use fleet portal battery telemetry to track actual performance per route.

Attachment type

Plow, broom, mower deck, and material application systems each have different draw profiles. High-torque mowing at slope draws more power than straight-blade plowing on flat grade.

Snow depth and density

Wet, dense, or compacted snow increases drive load and plow resistance. Deep accumulation per pass increases draw compared to light powder.

Surface condition

Ice, wet pavement, loose gravel, mud, and rough terrain increase traction effort and drive power consumption.

Slope

Uphill segments, slope mowing, and repeated grade transitions increase motor load substantially.

Material load

Loaded granular spreader or liquid tank increases platform weight and drive power demand throughout the route.

Route geometry

Routes with frequent direction changes, tight turns, and short segments increase cumulative energy per mile compared to long straight passes.

Stop frequency

High stop frequency from obstacle events, traffic interaction, or frequent dispatch pauses increases overall power draw.

Ambient temperature

Cold temperatures below freezing reduce LFP battery output capacity and increase power demand for all drive systems. Runtime is shorter in the coldest operating conditions.

Travel distance

Total route length, including transit to and from dock, contributes to total mission energy draw.

Speed and mode

Line-of-sight repositioning at up to 5–6 mph draws more power than sustained 2 mph autonomous operation.

05 — Auto-Return

The robot manages its own return to base.

When battery state drops to the configured low-battery threshold, the robot pauses its current mission, notifies the supervisor through the fleet portal, and returns to its geotagged dock or staging zone for charging. This also applies on communication loss and configured fault conditions.

Auto-return triggerRobot behavior
Low battery thresholdMission paused, supervisor notified, return to geotagged dock initiated
Communication lossAuto-return to dock, awaits reconnection
Configured fault conditionMission paused, return to dock, supervisor alert dispatched
Mission completeReturn to dock or staging position, proof-of-service record closed
