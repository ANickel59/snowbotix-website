Technology Specifications / Deployment Requirements

Build the operating plan before the robot runs.

Snowbotix does not deploy to an unmapped site and start running. Every deployment follows a structured process: site survey, route mapping, boundaries and exclusions, power logistics, and safety review, all approved before the first autonomous mission.

01 — Why Deployment Requires Preparation

Autonomous outdoor maintenance is site-specific by design.

Snowbotix operates within defined, pre-mapped zones with approved routes, geofenced boundaries, and human oversight. None of this can be set up remotely from a catalog description. The terrain, surface conditions, route geometry, pedestrian patterns, power access, crew responsibilities, and local requirements at your site determine how the system is configured.

Deployments that skip site preparation produce operational gaps: unmapped hazards, incorrectly set exclusion zones, mismatched attachment configurations, inadequate power logistics, and undefined supervision responsibilities. The deployment process exists to prevent those gaps, not to add administrative friction.

02 — 10-Step Pre-Deployment Process

From site assessment to first autonomous mission.

01

Site assessment and feasibility review

Physical walk-through of the site. Route geometry, surface conditions, terrain, slopes, exclusion candidates, pedestrian and vehicle traffic patterns, and power access documented. Route feasibility confirmed before further planning proceeds.

02

Operating zone definition

Authorized operating zones defined with GPS coordinates and physical boundary markers. Zones are documented, reviewed, and approved before any mapping work begins.

03

Exclusion zone mapping

Areas where the robot must not operate are identified and mapped: pedestrian-only zones, infrastructure exclusions, loading dock active areas, emergency access requirements, and any site-specific no-go areas. Exclusion zones are programmed into the navigation system before deployment.

04

Route programming and review

Approved routes are programmed into the fleet portal. Pass patterns, turn geometry, priority zones, and route sequencing are defined and reviewed by the operator before authorization.

05

Attachment selection and configuration

Attachments are selected based on route requirements, surface type, seasonal work scope, slope, and material logistics. Configuration is reviewed against route geometry and confirmed before deployment.

06

Power logistics planning

Charging dock location, power circuit requirements, hot-swap battery staging, and wireless dock setup are confirmed. Power access must be available at the planned dock location before go-live. 110V circuit required for wireless dock charging.

07

Supervisor and crew roles defined

Supervision staffing, crew coordination protocols, on-site E-stop responsibility, physical E-stop locations, and alert routing are documented and confirmed. Role-based portal access is provisioned before operation begins.

08

Safety and communication protocol review

On-site pedestrian and vehicle coordination protocols, hazard escalation procedures, communication contacts, and operating window schedules reviewed with the site team before the first mission.

09

Supervised test run

First mission executed under direct on-site supervision. Route, boundaries, exclusion zones, speed, sensing, alert routing, and proof-of-service record generation verified before unsupervised scheduled operation is authorized.

10

Deployment authorization and go-live

All checklist items confirmed. Deployment authorized. Scheduled and on-demand missions enabled. Supervisor monitoring responsibilities active from the first authorized mission.

03 — Deployment Models

Common deployment configurations.

On-Site Staging

Dedicated dock at a fixed site

Robot is permanently staged at one facility. Wireless dock charging overnight or between shifts. Suited for campuses, hospitals, government facilities, and logistics yards with consistent recurring routes.

Storm-Response Staging

Positioned for rapid dispatch when conditions trigger

Robot is staged at or near the site before a storm event. Up to 60 days standby capability supports pre-positioning before storm season. Auto-dispatch when weather conditions meet defined thresholds.

Persistent Patrol

Continuous or near-continuous cycling

Robot runs recurring passes across approved routes during defined operating windows. Dock return between pass cycles for charging or swap. Suited for airport ground-side, logistics yard, and campus walkway applications.

Demo / Site Test

Bounded first deployment to validate site fit

Defined scope — typically one or two routes — under direct supervision for initial service period. Route performance, proof-of-service records, and operational fit reviewed before scale decisions are made.

Clearance heightsOverhead clearances for robot and attachment confirmed on all route segments

Confirm before first autonomous operation.

Site walk-through completed

Operating zones defined and GPS-bounded

Exclusion zones mapped and programmed

Routes programmed and reviewed in fleet portal

Route geometry confirmed — width, turns, clearances

Slope at or within confirmed operating limit

Attachment selected and configured

Material compatibility validated (if applicable)

Power access confirmed at dock location

Charging infrastructure installed and tested

Supervisor trained and fleet portal access provisioned

On-site crew E-stop locations and protocols confirmed

Alert routing configured and tested

Safety and pedestrian coordination protocols communicated

Supervised test run completed and reviewed

Deployment authorized by site lead and fleet administrator
