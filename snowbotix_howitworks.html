01: Getting Started

Start with one high-value route.

Deployment begins with a single route, not a full site overhaul. The goal at the start is to identify the route that is most operationally disruptive or difficult to staff reliably. From there, the path moves to assessment, mapping, and supervised deployment.

High-value routes

Routes that cause the most operational friction

The best starting route is one where delay, inconsistency, or missed service has a clear downstream cost: a loading dock that backs up after a snowstorm, a retention pond that gets deferred each cycle, a campus perimeter no contractor reliably covers.

What to share

An aerial image or site map is enough to start

Send Snowbotix a route description, aerial image, or site map. Include terrain type, route frequency, current staffing approach, and known access or staging constraints. Snowbotix will review before recommending an approach.

What happens next

Snowbotix reviews the route before committing to anything

Not every route is a good fit on the first pass. Snowbotix will confirm whether the terrain, access, staging, and operating conditions support autonomous deployment before moving forward.

02: Deployment Process

Five steps from site assessment to robot going to work.

01

Show the site

Share the route, terrain photos, site map, or aerial image. Snowbotix reviews access, staging, terrain, and operating constraints before moving to the next step.

02

Deployment plan

Snowbotix prepares a deployment plan specific to the site: route boundaries, exclusion zones, recovery points, charging setup, and escalation protocols.

03

Team review

Your facilities team reviews and confirms the deployment plan. Any site-specific constraints, safety requirements, or operational rules are incorporated before launch.

04

Configure and deploy

Snowbotix maps the route, configures the system, and deploys the robot with all safety systems, monitoring, and supervisory protocols active.

05

Robot goes to work

The robot operates autonomously within approved route boundaries. Human supervisors monitor via the command-and-control workflow. Your team manages the operation, not the equipment.

04: Site Assessment & Mapping

Every deployment begins with a confirmed site assessment, not an assumption.

Assessment

What Snowbotix evaluates before deployment

  • Terrain type and grade
  • Surface conditions (pavement, turf, gravel, slope, mixed)
  • Route boundaries and feasible operating area
  • Exclusion zones: restricted areas, pedestrian paths, infrastructure
  • Staging, charging, and equipment recovery locations
  • Transition zones between route segments
  • Seasonal or environmental conditions affecting operating plans
  • Access restrictions, scheduled events, and site-specific rules
Route Mapping

What mapping produces before the robot runs

  • Defined operating boundary for the autonomous route
  • GPS-confirmed geofenced boundaries
  • Recovery points and charging stops built into the route
  • Exclusion zones programmed as hard boundaries
  • Alternate paths for blocked segments
  • Integration with ArcGIS, GeoJSON, or existing site data
  • Reviewed and confirmed by your team before first run
05: Site Readiness

What a site needs before deployment can start.

Physical Access

Route access and staging

The robot needs a way to get to the route, a place to stage and charge, and a defined recovery point in case of obstruction or exception. Most sites that already support grounds equipment can accommodate this.

Route Definition

A mappable route with clear boundaries

Autonomous operation requires a defined operating area. Snowbotix can map routes from satellite imagery, site plans, or field walkthroughs. The route needs a boundary that can be geofenced and exclusion zones that can be programmed.

Operations

Contact and notification protocol

Your team needs a designated point of contact for the deployment plan review and a way to notify Snowbotix of site changes, events, or access restrictions that affect the operating schedule.

06: Controlled Operation & Safety

Autonomous does not mean unsupervised.

Every Snowbotix deployment runs with active human supervision. The robot operates within a programmed route boundary. Snowbotix monitors via a command-and-control workflow and manages exceptions in real time.

On-Robot Safety Systems
  • 360-degree obstacle detection
  • 6-foot safety bubble around the robot
  • Dual physical E-stops
  • Digital kill switch via remote supervisors
  • HD cameras and thermal sensing
  • LiDAR
  • RTK-GNSS for positioning and boundary enforcement
  • Automatic stop on obstruction or out-of-bounds detection
Human Supervision & Escalation
  • Snowbotix remote supervisors monitor active routes
  • Command-and-control workflow with live camera feeds
  • Obstructions, pedestrians, and anomalies escalate to the supervisor
  • Supervisor reviews and either clears the path or pauses the route
  • Client team receives alerts for site conditions that require on-site attention
  • All exceptions, stops, and escalations are logged in the service record
Operating Protocols
  • Robots operate within confirmed geofenced boundaries only
  • Exclusion zones are programmed and enforced at the hardware level
  • Speed limits: up to 2 mph autonomous, 5-6 mph line-of-sight
  • Safety zone activates around any detected person, vehicle, or object
  • Scheduled downtime for events, facility access, and maintenance windows
Restricted Areas
  • Any area can be designated as an exclusion zone during route mapping
  • Exclusion zones are geofenced and enforced before and during operation
  • Changes to restricted areas are updated in the route map before the next run
  • Your team can add or modify exclusions at any time via the deployment plan review process

12: Common Questions

How buyers typically evaluate Snowbotix.

How does the robot know where to go?
The robot operates on a pre-mapped route defined during the site assessment and deployment planning process. Routes are geofenced using GPS and RTK-GNSS, and the robot uses onboard sensing to navigate within the approved boundary. It does not learn routes autonomously or navigate outside defined boundaries without human approval. Every route is confirmed by your team before the first run.
Can certain areas be permanently restricted?
Yes. Any area can be designated as an exclusion zone during route mapping. Exclusion zones are geofenced and enforced at the hardware level before and during every operation. They can include sensitive infrastructure, pedestrian areas, access-controlled zones, or any other designated restricted space. Your team can add or modify exclusion zones at any time via the deployment plan review process, and the changes are incorporated into the route map before the next run.
What happens when the robot encounters a person?
The robot automatically stops when a person is detected within its 6-foot safety bubble. 360-degree obstacle detection and dual E-stops are active at all times during operation. If the path remains blocked, the exception is escalated to a Snowbotix remote supervisor who reviews the situation via live camera feeds and determines how to proceed. The person does not need to take any action. All stop events and escalations are logged in the service record.
How does charging work?
The robot supports a 1-minute hot-swap battery for fast field charging and is also compatible with a wireless dock charging setup. Charging stations are incorporated into the deployment plan during site assessment. Snowbotix handles battery management and charging logistics as part of the service. Recovery points within the route are configured so the robot can return to the charging location as part of its standard operating pattern.
What happens if the robot gets stuck or has a mechanical issue?
Snowbotix remote supervisors monitor active routes in real time. If the robot encounters a situation it cannot resolve autonomously (stuck on terrain, mechanical issue, blocked path), the supervisor is immediately notified and manages the exception. In cases requiring on-site intervention, Snowbotix coordinates the response. All stop events, escalations, and resolution outcomes are logged in the service record. Equipment maintenance and operational recovery are Snowbotix's responsibility under the RaaS model.
