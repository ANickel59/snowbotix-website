2. The operational problem

Sweeping looks simple until it becomes constant.

Sand, salt, leaves, litter, dirt, and surface buildup return across the same walkways, curb lines, parking edges, campus paths, and public-facing entrances. When teams are short-staffed, these routes get pushed behind higher-priority work. The result is visible deterioration: messy entrances, uneven campus appearance, harder-to-maintain pedestrian routes, and more manual cleanup hours.

For large sites, the issue is not whether someone can sweep. The issue is whether the work can be repeated, scheduled, verified, and kept off the critical path for already-stretched facilities teams.

Where manual sweeping breaks down

Recurring routes compete with urgent work orders.
Post-winter sand and salt cleanup stretches across large surface areas.
Leaves, litter, and debris return faster than teams can clear them.
Public-facing entrances need cleaner surfaces before peak arrival windows.
Facilities leaders have limited visibility into what was actually completed.
Contracted work can be difficult to verify without route-level documentation.

5. Attachments / sensing / operating model

Attachments

Snowbotix can be configured with modular attachments for sweeping and debris work, including power broom and vacuum-oriented configurations where appropriate. Final attachment selection depends on surface type, debris profile, route width, access constraints, and collection requirements.

Sensing

The robot uses 360-degree sensing to detect people, vehicles, obstacles, and route obstructions. When an obstruction prevents completion, the system can pause, alert a human supervisor, and create a record for follow-up.

Operating model

Snowbotix starts with site assessment and route mapping. Approved paths are geofenced and programmed into the operating system. The robot can then run scheduled or on-demand sweeping missions under remote human supervision.

Operating modes

Autonomous mode for mapped recurring routes.
Line-of-sight mode for repositioning, loading, or complex maneuvers.
Remote monitoring for mission oversight, pause, and override.
Fleet dashboard visibility for route progress, status, and battery level.

6. Proof and reporting

Sweeping is easier to manage when the work creates a record.

Snowbotix creates proof-of-service records that show where the robot operated, when the route was completed, and what conditions or obstructions were encountered. This gives facilities, operations, and vendor-management teams a clearer view of recurring outdoor maintenance.

Reporting can include:

GPS route trace
Mission start and stop time
Completed route history
Before/after geotagged images or video
Area covered
Battery and runtime data
Obstacle and pause events
Supervisor notes or exception records
Exportable records for internal review, work orders, or service verification

Strong fit

Likely not a fit

Residential properties.
One-time cleanup projects.
Loose, undefined, or constantly changing work areas.
Hazardous material cleanup.
Heavy waste removal or bulk dumping.
High-speed public road sweeping.
Sites without a workable staging, charging, or supervision model.
Routes with crowd conditions that prevent predictable autonomous operation.
