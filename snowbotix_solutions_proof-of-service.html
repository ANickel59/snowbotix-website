Document every autonomous maintenance route.

01 — The Verification Problem

Outdoor work breaks down when the record is incomplete.

Large sites depend on recurring maintenance across sidewalks, paths, plazas, entrances, lots, curbs, slopes, rights-of-way, and controlled zones. Once that work spreads across crews, vendors, shifts, weather events, and multiple properties, leaders are left piecing together updates from texts, radio calls, manual logs, vendor notes, and spot checks.

Facilities and operations teams need to know what was serviced, where the work happened, when it was completed, what conditions were present, and what still needs attention. Snowbotix turns autonomous maintenance into a documented operating record, so route-level service is visible without walking every path, entrance, yard, or perimeter.

Questions Snowbotix answers:

  • Which route was completed?
  • When did service start and stop?
  • What areas were skipped, blocked, or interrupted?
  • What conditions were present during the run?
  • What did the surface look like before and after service?
  • What follow-up work needs attention?
  • How often are priority routes being serviced?
  • What happened when the robot encountered an obstruction?

03 — Mission Records and Route Status

See where the robot operated, not just where service was scheduled.

A scheduled route does not prove a completed route. Snowbotix reporting shows the actual operating path, route progress, mission history, and completion status so teams can confirm work across distributed outdoor zones. GPS traces show where the robot traveled. Mission records show when the work happened. Route history shows what was completed over time.

Scope note

Snowbotix proof-of-service records support internal operational review and service verification. They do not defeat legal claims, reduce insurance premiums, or satisfy FAA, OSHA, or ADA compliance requirements. Use the records to support internal reporting, work order review, vendor management, and operational planning.

06 — From Routes to Records

Five steps from mapped routes to service documentation.

01

Map the site

Snowbotix maps routes, zones, access points, surfaces, obstacles, and priority areas that define the operating environment

02

Define reporting requirements

Team defines what needs to be documented: route completion, before/after media, sensor data, exception events, supervisor notes, area covered, export requirements, and review cadence

03

Run autonomous missions

Snowbotix operates on approved routes using supervised autonomy, 360-degree sensing, and human-in-the-loop monitoring

04

Capture proof-of-service records

System records mission history, GPS traces, visual evidence, sensor context, route exceptions, and supervisor notes

05

Review, export, and act

Teams use the dashboard and reports to verify service, review exceptions, update work orders, and plan follow-up maintenance

07 — Site Fit

Best for sites where service verification matters.

Strong fit

  • Commercial campuses with recurring outdoor maintenance routes
  • Healthcare, education, logistics, industrial, utility, municipal, aviation, or government sites
  • Sidewalks, plazas, paths, curbs, yards, parking areas, trails, or controlled access zones
  • Teams that manage internal crews, outside vendors, or distributed maintenance routes
  • Organizations that need route-level service records
  • Sites where missed work disrupts operations, access, or public-facing conditions
  • Teams that need reporting for operations, finance, sustainability, or vendor management

Likely not a fit

  • Residential properties
  • One-time service with no recurring route
  • Indoor-only maintenance
  • Sites without a practical mapping, staging, charging, or supervision model
  • Routes that change too frequently to support reliable reporting
  • Work requiring compliance-grade audit, certified inspection, or legal documentation
