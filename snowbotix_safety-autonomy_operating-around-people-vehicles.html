Operating Around People & Vehicles

Controlled autonomy for active outdoor sites.

Commercial sites are not empty. They have pedestrians, employees, maintenance crews, deliveries, service vehicles, and shift changes happening around the same routes the robot is meant to maintain. Snowbotix is not designed for controlled test environments. It is designed for sites where people and vehicles are already there — with operating controls that account for that from the start.

01 — Speed Governance

Autonomous operating speeds are governed, not variable.

Speed governance is not a software setting that can be turned up. Autonomous operation is governed to a controlled speed that reflects the reality of working in environments where people are present.

Autonomous mode

2 mph

Maximum governed speed for autonomous route operation. Consistent across all commercial and institutional site types.

Line-of-sight / manual mode

5 mph

Maximum speed when a local operator controls the unit directly. Used for repositioning, loading, docking, and complex maneuvers.

02 — Operating Controls

Separate operating controls for pedestrians and vehicles.

Pedestrian operating controls

Applied wherever people are present on or near the route

360-degree pedestrian detection

Sensors detect people around the unit, not just in the forward travel direction. The perimeter is active during autonomous operation.

Automatic halt when person detected

When a person is detected within the sensing radius, the unit stops autonomously. It does not continue. The event is logged.

Route scheduling for off-peak windows

High-foot-traffic areas can be scheduled for off-peak or overnight operation windows when pedestrian activity is lower.

Signage and site communication

Site teams are advised to communicate robot operating windows to employees and visitors for high-activity zones.

Supervisor escalation path

Any pedestrian-related stop event can be escalated by the remote supervisor for facility notification or rerouting.

Vehicle operating controls

Applied at routes shared with trucks, service vehicles, and equipment

Vehicle detection and halt

Vehicles within the sensing perimeter trigger a halt. The event is logged and escalated to the supervisor for review.

Line-of-sight manual in active vehicle areas

Route segments with active or unpredictable vehicle traffic are handled through line-of-sight manual operation, not autonomous mode.

Geofenced separation of vehicle and pedestrian zones

Operating boundaries separate areas of active vehicle movement from pedestrian or lower-traffic routes.

Time-based operating windows

Dock lanes, delivery areas, and loading zones are scheduled to operate during defined windows outside peak vehicle traffic.

Site planning input on vehicle flow

Truck routes, shift change patterns, and delivery windows are mapped during site assessment and reflected in operating boundaries.

04 — Crew Coordination

Working alongside maintenance crews, not around them.

Snowbotix does not operate independently from the people managing the site. Deployment works better when robot operating windows are communicated to maintenance crews, facility management, and supervisors who can plan around them.

Crew typeCoordination approach
Grounds maintenance crewsSchedule robot routes during windows when crews are not working the same zones; use line-of-sight manual when adjacent work is underway
Delivery and logistics teamsMap dock and delivery route segments for off-peak windows; use manual mode for dock-adjacent work during active shift hours
Building maintenance and contractorsFlag active work zones as temporary exclusions; supervisor holds robot at boundary until zone is clear

05 — Site Planning Inputs

People-flow and vehicle-flow information improves deployment design.

The more accurately the site assessment captures how people and vehicles actually move through the property, the better the routes, boundaries, and operating windows can be planned. Useful inputs include:

People flow

Peak foot traffic hours, shift change times, public access patterns, primary pedestrian routes, and high-visibility public zones.

Vehicle flow

Truck delivery schedules, internal service vehicle routes, shift-change traffic, parking lot peak hours, and dock assignment patterns.

Operating windows

Off-peak hours, overnight windows, early-morning access before tenant or visitor arrival, and shift-gap timing for schedule-based routes.

FAQ

Questions about operating in active environments.

How fast does the robot move near people?

During autonomous operation, the robot is governed to 2 mph. During line-of-sight manual operation — where a local operator controls the unit directly — the maximum speed is 5 mph. There is no mode that removes speed governance.

What happens when the robot detects someone?

When a person is detected within the sensing perimeter, the unit stops and holds position. The event is logged with location, timestamp, and sensor data. The remote supervisor reviews the event and determines whether to hold, reroute, or switch to manual operation.

Can the robot operate in a location that is always busy?

High-foot-traffic areas are best handled through scheduled operating windows — overnight, early morning, or shift-gap timing when pedestrian activity is lower. Routes that cannot be scheduled around consistent activity may be identified as candidates for line-of-sight manual operation or exclusion.

What happens if a truck pulls into the operating zone?

Vehicle detection triggers an automatic halt. The supervisor reviews the event and decides whether to hold the robot at its current position, defer the route segment, or switch to manual mode to handle the nearby work.

Does Snowbotix work in truck yards and active logistics facilities?

Snowbotix can operate in logistics environments with careful route design. Pedestrian corridors and access paths are typically the best starting routes. Active dock lanes and vehicle-heavy areas are handled through line-of-sight manual operation and time-based scheduling rather than autonomous operation.

How are crews told that the robot is operating?

Site communication is set up during deployment planning. This typically includes facility notices, signage at the operating area, and direct coordination with maintenance, security, and facilities management teams. The supervisor can also notify facility contacts directly from the monitoring dashboard.

Does Snowbotix work when there are contractors on site?

Temporary work zones from contractors can be added as time-limited exclusions in the site map. The supervisor holds the robot at the boundary of any active contractor work and can route around the area or defer the segment until the zone is clear.

Who is responsible for telling people a robot is operating?

Site communication is the responsibility of the facility operator and the deployment team, not the robot. The supervisor supports escalation and facility notification, but operational communication — signage, employee briefings, and contractor coordination — is a site management function.
