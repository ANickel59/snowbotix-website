# 3. The Snowbotix Deployment Path

## Section headline

A practical path from route to robot operations.

## Body

Snowbotix deployment follows a clear operating path: define the work, plan the route, prepare the site, validate the operating map, configure mission logic, launch supervised missions, monitor activity, and review service records.

## Process cards

### 1. Assess the work

Snowbotix reviews the route, zone, site, or recurring outdoor task to understand whether it can be mapped, supported, and operated with supervised autonomy.

### 2. Prepare the site

Snowbotix identifies what the deployment needs: robot home, storage, charging or battery workflow, access, materials, and local coordination.

### 3. Validate the route

Snowbotix confirms approved routes, geofenced areas, exclusion zones, service areas, staging points, and exception protocols before active missions begin.

### 4. Configure mission starts

Missions may be human-started, scheduled, weather-informed, weather-triggered, or hybrid.

### 5. Run supervised operations

The robot operates on approved routes while Snowbotix human-in-the-loop supervisors monitor activity and review exceptions.

### 6. Report and refine

Snowbotix provides service records, reviews operating history, refines route logic, and recommends whether additional routes, attachments, robots, or sites should be added.

Suggested flow:

# 4. Site Assessment & Pre-Deployment Planning

# 5. Site Readiness, Robot Home & Storage

## Section headline

Every deployment needs a clear place for the robot to work, wait, charge, and be serviced.

## Body

Snowbotix works with each customer to define the physical setup required for deployment. The right setup depends on the route, mission frequency, attachment mix, charging model, material use, site security, and whether the deployment is a short pilot or a longer-term operating program.

## Robot home

Each deployment needs a designated robot home: a known location where the robot can be staged, parked, charged, serviced, or sent when it is not actively working.

For a short pilot, this may be as simple as an indoor space, secured parking spot, or maintenance area. For a longer-term deployment, Snowbotix may recommend a sheltered robot home, maintenance bay, carport, storage shed, Conex-style container, or base-station area.

## Storage and staging considerations

Snowbotix reviews:

- Robot parking or staging location
- Indoor, covered, secured, or weather-protected storage options
- Charging access or battery-swap workflow
- Room for attachments, batteries, and service access
- Salt, liquid de-icer, or other consumable storage where relevant
- Safe travel path between the robot home and service route
- Access for Snowbotix technicians or designated site staff
- Gate access, after-hours access, and site security requirements

## Charging and battery workflow

Depending on the deployment, Snowbotix may configure plug-in charging, battery swaps, wireless charging, or a hybrid approach. Battery swaps and refills can be assigned to Snowbotix, trained customer staff, or an agreed operating partner depending on the service model.

## Consumables

For snow and ice workflows, the site may need a defined process for salt, liquid de-icer, or other material refills. Snowbotix defines how refills are handled before missions begin.

# 7. How Missions Start

## Section headline

Missions start by human direction, schedule, weather signal, or approved rules.

## Body

Snowbotix does not ask customers to hand over judgment to a machine. Each mission starts from an approved route, defined boundaries, configured service rules, and a clear trigger for when work should begin.

Mission-start logic is configured around the site, route, task, and service window. Some buyers start with human-started or scheduled missions, then add weather-informed or weather-triggered workflows as the route and operating rules become repeatable.

## Mission-start options

### Human-started

A designated customer user or Snowbotix supervisor starts the mission.

Best for:

- First deployments
- Pilot routes
- High-traffic areas
- Sites still validating operating rules

### Scheduled

The robot runs an approved route during a planned service window.

Best for:

- Routine sweeping
- Planned mowing
- Recurring inspection routes
- Pre-shift or overnight service windows
- Standard winter operations

### Weather-informed

Snowbotix uses weather or site-condition signals to recommend a mission, with human confirmation before the robot starts.

Best for:

- Anti-icing
- De-icing
- Snow response planning
- Sites that want automation support without automatic deployment

### Weather-triggered

Approved rules can start or queue a mission when defined thresholds are met.

Best for:

- Mature routes
- Defined service thresholds
- Repeated winter workflows
- Sites with clear operating rules and escalation paths

### Hybrid

Schedule, weather logic, and human confirmation work together.

Best for:

- Hospitals
- Airports
- Campuses
- Public works routes
- Industrial sites
- Multi-zone facilities

This is a core trust point. Snowbotix is not simply handing over autonomous equipment and asking the customer to manage it alone.

- Route pauses, restarts, or reroutes
- Remote troubleshooting
- Service-alert review
- Customer coordination
- Maintenance escalation
- Route refinement

# 12. Pilot-to-Scale Success Path

## Section headline

Start with one route. Expand when operating evidence supports it.

## Body

Most deployments should begin with a defined route, zone, or recurring task. Snowbotix uses that first deployment to validate the map, mission rules, site readiness, service records, exception handling, and operating workflow before recommending expansion.

## Phase 1: Test & Validate

Begin with a defined route, zone, or recurring task. Validate the operating map, geofenced boundaries, mission-start logic, supervision workflow, battery or refill process, and service records.

## Phase 2: Expand

Add additional routes, attachments, seasons, or robots when the first deployment proves repeatable.

## Phase 3: Generate Leverage

Scale across larger sites, portfolios, districts, or multi-robot operations when the operating model, support model, and reporting structure are ready.

## Expansion signals

- The route can be repeated.
- The mission-start logic is clear.
- Exceptions are manageable.
- The robot home and storage workflow are practical.
- The team understands the workflow.
- Service records support review and refinement.
- The same model applies to additional routes, tasks, or sites.
