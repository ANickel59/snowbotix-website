## Section 1: The parks maintenance problem

## Public parks are open when staffing is thinnest.

Parks departments manage some of the most visible outdoor spaces in a municipality. The work is distributed, seasonal, weather-dependent, and public. A missed route does not stay invisible for long.

Hard-to-staff in parks and recreation usually means:

- **Early access routes:** sidewalks, parking approaches, recreation center entries, public bathrooms, trailheads, and transit connectors that need to be ready before peak use.
- **Spread-out assets:** multiple parks, fields, plazas, and trail loops competing for the same crews and equipment.
- **Seasonal spikes:** winter events, spring cleanup, summer mowing, fall debris, and tournament or event weekends that overload the same team.
- **High-visibility complaints:** a blocked entry, icy path, overgrown slope, or debris-covered plaza quickly becomes a resident issue.
- **Risky terrain:** slopes, berms, retention ponds, drainage areas, and perimeter edges that are hard to mow consistently with human-operated equipment.
- **Difficult proof:** crews may do the work, but completed service can be hard to verify across a dispersed park system.

Snowbotix gives parks and public works teams a persistent outdoor maintenance asset for recurring routes that are hard to staff, easy to defer, and important to document.

## Section 3: High-fit parks and recreation use cases

## Start where access, visibility, and staffing pressure overlap.

### Recreation centers and aquatic facilities

Keep entry walks, parking approaches, loading areas, staff access points, and public gathering zones on a defined service schedule.

**Best-fit routes:** main entrances, accessible paths, drop-off zones, public restroom approaches, pool or rink access routes, staff walkways, and perimeter sidewalks.

**Why it matters:** Recreation facilities concentrate public expectations. Snow, ice, debris, and overgrown edges are noticed quickly because these sites are used by families, seniors, youth programs, and scheduled groups.

### Trailheads and paved park loops

Automate defined routes where residents arrive early and expect access before crews can reach every park.

**Best-fit routes:** paved trailheads, connector sidewalks, parking-to-trail approaches, bridge approaches, comfort-station access, and short priority loops.

**Why it matters:** Trail systems create distributed maintenance demand. Snowbotix helps parks teams prioritize the routes that determine whether a park feels usable.

### Athletic complexes and tournament sites

Support event readiness before, during, and after high-traffic usage windows.

**Best-fit routes:** spectator walkways, concession approaches, plaza surfaces, parking connectors, field-house entries, debris routes, and post-event sweeping.

**Why it matters:** Athletic complexes create weekend spikes that compete with routine maintenance. Snowbotix can handle recurring sweep and access routes while crews focus on field conditions, event support, and repairs.

### Retention ponds, berms, and perimeter slopes

Use robotic slope mowing and brush control where manual work is physically difficult, deferred, or inconsistently staffed.

**Best-fit routes:** detention and retention pond slopes, drainage embankments, fence lines, berms, perimeter edges, and rough-cut zones.

**Why it matters:** Slopes are often the work no one wants to assign. Snowbotix reduces dependence on crews for high-friction mowing routes and helps keep public areas visibly maintained.

### Civic plazas, memorial parks, and event grounds

Maintain high-visibility public spaces with repeatable sweeping, snow, and debris workflows.

**Best-fit routes:** plaza edges, monument approaches, municipal campus paths, festival routes, transit-adjacent sidewalks, and pedestrian corridors.

**Why it matters:** These spaces carry civic expectation. Service gaps are visible, photographed, and escalated quickly.

## Section 4: The proof layer parks teams can use

## Document the work without sending a supervisor to every site.

Parks and public works leaders need to know what was serviced, when it was serviced, and what the route looked like before and after work was completed.

Snowbotix creates a documented operating record using:

- **Geocoded before-and-after imagery:** visual confirmation tied to route, time, and location.
- **Mission history:** completed route records, deployment timing, runtime, and service status.
- **GPS route tracking:** visibility into where each unit operated and which zones were covered.
- **Area-serviced reporting:** square footage, acres, or route segments completed, depending on the mapped mission.
- **Sensor-supported context:** site-level weather and surface-condition data that helps explain when and why a route was triggered.
- **Supervisor escalation records:** issues such as fallen limbs, blocked paths, unauthorized vehicles, or unexpected obstructions can be captured and routed for follow-up.

For a parks department, this changes the conversation from “Did someone get to that site?” to “Here is the route record, timestamp, and visual evidence.”

## Section 5: Controlled autonomy for public spaces

## Autonomous does not mean unsupervised.

Snowbotix is built for commercial and public infrastructure environments where people, vehicles, weather, and site conditions change.

The operating model includes:

- **360-degree sensing** to detect people, vehicles, and obstacles around the unit.
- **Six-foot safety radius** that triggers an automatic halt when a person or object enters the defined zone.
- **Human-in-the-loop monitoring** for real-time oversight and escalation.
- **Geofenced boundaries** that keep each mission inside approved operating areas.
- **Mapped exclusion zones** around playgrounds, splash pads, event areas, sensitive landscapes, or other restricted spaces.
- **Low-speed autonomous operation** for unmanned public-space routes, with line-of-sight manual mode available for repositioning or high-complexity areas.

This is a controlled outdoor maintenance workflow, not an unattended gadget placed into a public park.

## Section 8: Pilot-to-scale path

## Start with one route that makes the value visible.

A parks pilot should be narrow enough to control, but visible enough to matter.

**Recommended pilot site:** one recreation center, trailhead, athletic complex, or civic plaza with a defined route and repeat service need.

**Pilot route examples:**

- Recreation center entry path + accessible parking connector + public restroom approach.
- Trailhead parking approach + paved trail loop segment + comfort station route.
- Athletic complex pedestrian route + concessions approach + post-event sweeping path.
- Retention pond slope + adjacent path + recurring mowing route.

**Pilot process:**

1. **Select the route:** Identify the park, facility, or route where staffing, visibility, and service frequency overlap.
2. **Map the site:** Define boundaries, operating windows, exclusion zones, staging areas, and service priorities.
3. **Run controlled missions:** Deploy Snowbotix on approved routes with human-in-the-loop monitoring.
4. **Review service evidence:** Evaluate before/after imagery, mission logs, operating time, area serviced, and crew feedback.
5. **Decide expansion:** Add routes, parks, or seasonal use cases once the operating model is validated.

## Section 10: FAQ

### Does Snowbotix replace parks crews?

Snowbotix is designed to automate recurring outdoor routes that are hard to staff, repetitive, or physically demanding. Parks crews still handle higher-judgment work, event needs, repairs, inspections, public interactions, and tasks outside the mapped operating route.

### Can Snowbotix operate around park visitors?

Snowbotix uses 360-degree sensing, a six-foot safety radius, geofenced routes, low-speed autonomous operation, and human-in-the-loop monitoring. Public-space deployment should be planned around approved routes, operating windows, and site-specific exclusion zones.

### Is Snowbotix for natural trails?

Snowbotix is best suited for defined, maintained routes such as paved trails, park loops, plazas, sidewalks, trailheads, parking approaches, turf zones, slopes, berms, and service roads. A site assessment determines whether a specific trail or route is appropriate.

### How does Snowbotix help during winter?

Snowbotix can support snow removal, power brooming, de-icing, pretreatment, and priority-route clearing. Onboard sensing and site-level weather context help determine when routes should be serviced, while proof-of-service records document completed work.

### How does this help with resident complaints?

Snowbotix creates route-level service records, including geocoded before-and-after imagery and mission history. That gives parks and public works teams a clearer record of what was serviced, where, and when.

### What does the site need before deployment?

A strong deployment needs a mapped route, staging or charging area, approved operating windows, defined exclusion zones, and coordination with parks, public works, safety, and procurement stakeholders.

### What is the budget model?

Snowbotix can be evaluated through pilot, Robot-as-a-Service, lease, direct purchase, or contractor-supported deployment structures depending on the public budget path. The strongest case usually starts with a specific route, documented staffing pressure, and a multi-season service plan.
