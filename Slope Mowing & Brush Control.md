## The operational problem

### Vegetation work gets deferred where terrain, staffing, and visibility break down.

Most grounds teams can keep visible lawns on schedule. The harder problem sits at the edge of the site: steep banks, retention ponds, fence lines, access roads, trail edges, curbs, rights-of-way, drainage areas, and rough-cut zones.

These routes are recurring and operationally important, but they are often difficult to staff, difficult to verify, and easy to postpone. When vegetation work falls behind, teams face blocked sightlines, poor site appearance, drainage concerns, access issues, complaints, and repeated manual coordination.

### Common failure points

Hard-to-staff terrain  
Slopes, embankments, pond edges, uneven ground, and rough vegetation require more planning than standard turf routes.

Deferred perimeter work  
Fence lines, remote gates, back-lot edges, curbs, and trail edges are easy to skip until overgrowth becomes visible or operationally disruptive.

Contractor variability  
Vegetation routes often depend on outsourced labor, seasonal availability, and inconsistent crew coverage.

Limited service visibility  
Grounds leaders may not know what was cut, what was skipped, or what new obstruction appeared unless someone physically checks the route.

Seasonal equipment inefficiency  
Many sites maintain separate equipment or service contracts for mowing, snow, sweeping, inspection, and reporting, even when the work follows repeatable outdoor routes.

#### Operating model

Snowbotix does not treat mowing automation as a generic drop-it-on-the-grass workflow.

Each deployment is built around:

Mapped route logic  
Field teams define mow zones, slope boundaries, exclusion areas, access paths, turn zones, and recovery points.

Autonomous execution  
The robot operates along approved routes for mowing, rough-cut work, brush control, or inspection passes.

Human-in-the-loop monitoring  
Remote supervisors monitor operations and escalate issues when the robot identifies an obstruction, blocked path, fallen limb, vehicle, person, or abnormal condition.

Fleet management visibility  
Facilities teams can review robot status, route history, deployment records, area coverage, and proof-of-service data.

## Site fit criteria

### Best fit: recurring vegetation routes with clear boundaries and operational value.

Snowbotix is strongest where vegetation work is repeatable, mapped, and important enough to document.

#### Strong fit

- Commercial, institutional, municipal, industrial, energy, airport, or government site
- Recurring mowing, slope, rough-cut, or brush-control routes
- Defined outdoor zones with clear operating boundaries
- Retention ponds, embankments, perimeters, fence lines, curbs, trails, rights-of-way, or remote access areas
- Labor shortages, contractor inconsistency, or deferred grounds work
- Site map, aerial image, or route sketch available
- Internal facilities or grounds owner available for assessment
- Feasible staging, charging, and recovery area
- Vegetation type and desired finish can be defined during assessment

#### May require further assessment

- Dense pedestrian activity near the route
- Mixed vehicle and robot movement zones
- Severe uneven terrain
- Unknown subsurface hazards
- Drainage areas with soft ground
- Heavy brush, saplings, or woody vegetation
- Sensitive habitat or regulated vegetation zones
- Remote sites with limited connectivity
- Slopes requiring special access, recovery, or safety planning

#### Not a fit

- Residential lawns
- One-time brush clearing with no recurring route
- Forest clearing or land-clearing work
- Routes with no feasible map boundary
- Terrain where the robot cannot be safely staged, supervised, or recovered
- Vegetation work that requires heavy forestry equipment
