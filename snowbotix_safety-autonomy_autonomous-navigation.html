Autonomous Navigation

Route-based autonomy for complex outdoor sites.

Snowbotix does not navigate by open-ended AI. Every deployment starts with site assessment, digital mapping, route approval, and facility review. The robot runs on mapped routes that reflect your access priorities, operating constraints, and site-specific traffic patterns. Approved means the people running the facility have reviewed what the robot will do before it starts work.

01 — How Navigation Is Built

Every route starts with a site review, not a default algorithm.

Snowbotix builds navigation from a structured process. The robot does not explore the site on arrival and figure out what to do. The site is assessed, mapped, reviewed, and approved before autonomous operation begins.

01

Site assessment

Routes, terrain gradients, traffic patterns, surface types, emergency access, docking and charging locations, and priority zone requirements are reviewed before any map is created.

02

Digital mapping

The site is converted into a digital map: route paths, geofenced boundaries, obstacle records, surface constraints, pile zones, and priority areas. The map reflects how the property actually operates.

03

Route planning

Routes are defined around what matters: emergency access paths, time-sensitive zones, foot traffic patterns, surface sensitivity, and overnight or off-peak access windows.

04

Facility review

Stakeholders review mapped routes, exclusion zones, and operating logic. The map is finalized with the input of the people who manage the property — not just the deployment team.

05

Route activation

Routes are activated after review and validation. A pilot route tests the operating model in one zone before additional routes are added.

02 — Operating Modes

Five modes for different site conditions and task types.

Navigation mode matches the task. Autonomous operation handles most mapped routes. Line-of-sight manual operation handles complex repositioning and high-interaction areas. Additional modes support specific scheduling and deployment patterns.

Mode 01

Autonomous

Operates on approved, mapped routes with geofencing, obstacle detection, and remote human-in-the-loop oversight. Handles the majority of route work.

Mode 02

Line-of-sight / manual

A local operator controls the unit directly. Used for loading, repositioning, docking, or higher-complexity maneuvers that benefit from direct human control.

Mode 03

Supervisor-assisted

A remote supervisor monitors work in progress and can intervene, reroute, or escalate. Used when conditions change or an event requires human review.

Mode 04

Scheduled

Routes are pre-planned for defined time windows — overnight runs, early morning access, off-peak gaps, or storm-response timing.

Mode 05

Event-triggered

Route activation responds to weather thresholds, accumulation data, facility alerts, or supervisor dispatch rather than a fixed schedule.

04 — Mission Visibility

Teams can see what the robot is doing and where.

The mission visibility portal gives teams real-time route progress, location data, and alerts during autonomous operation. Supervisors monitor from a remote dashboard, respond to events, review service records, and escalate to facility teams when needed.

Route progress tracking

Live route status, completed and incomplete segments, estimated finish time, and current unit location.

Event and alert feed

Stop events, obstacle detection triggers, geofence proximity alerts, and supervisor escalation queue.

Service record log

Timestamped route completion records, geocoded photos, area coverage data, and session notes.

Remote override capability

Supervisors can pause, reroute, switch to manual, or escalate to facility contacts directly from the dashboard.

FAQ

Questions about autonomous navigation.

Does the robot figure out the site on its own?

No. Every deployment starts with a site assessment and mapping process. Routes, operating zones, and exclusion areas are defined by the deployment team and reviewed by facility stakeholders before autonomous operation begins.

Who approves the routes before they are used?

Facility stakeholders review mapped routes, operating zones, exclusion areas, and route logic before activation. The goal is to ensure the map reflects how the property actually operates — not just the default path the robot would take.

Can routes be changed after deployment?

Yes. Routes can be adjusted based on service records, facility feedback, seasonal changes, or new access requirements. Changes go through review before activation.

What happens when the robot reaches the edge of an approved zone?

Geofenced boundaries keep the robot inside defined operating areas. When a boundary is reached, the unit follows the programmed response — which may include stopping, holding position, returning to dock, or escalating to a remote supervisor depending on context.

How does the robot handle areas with heavy pedestrian or vehicle traffic?

Routes through high-traffic areas are assessed during site review. Options include scheduling routes during off-peak windows, using line-of-sight manual operation for those segments, or defining exclusion zones during peak hours.

What is event-triggered mode?

Event-triggered mode activates a route in response to a threshold or alert rather than a fixed schedule. Common triggers include weather data, accumulation readings, or a supervisor dispatch. It is useful for storm response that needs to begin at a specific threshold rather than a fixed time.
