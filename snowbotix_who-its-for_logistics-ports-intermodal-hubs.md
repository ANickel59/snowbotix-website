## Section 1: The logistics operating problem

## In logistics, outdoor maintenance becomes operational when it touches throughput.

A blocked walkway, icy dock approach, uncleared gatehouse route, or debris-filled yard connector may look like a facilities issue. In a logistics environment, it can quickly become an operations issue.

Drivers still need to check in. Yard teams still need to move. Warehouse teams still need to reach the building before shift start. Security still needs a clear route. Supervisors still need documentation. And the facility cannot wait for a contractor who is already stretched across every other customer in the same storm.

In logistics, ports, and intermodal hubs, hard-to-staff outdoor work usually means:

### Work that happens before the shift starts

The facility needs dock approaches, gatehouse paths, parking connectors, employee walkways, and key building entries ready before people arrive. If work starts only after the first complaint, the site is already behind.

### Routes spread across large outdoor footprints

Distribution centers, ports, and intermodal hubs can have long distances between employee parking, gatehouses, dock areas, terminal offices, trailer yards, container yards, maintenance shops, and support buildings. The work is distributed, repetitive, and easy to under-document.

### Weather windows that arrive during peak operating pressure

Snow, ice, slush, wind, blowing debris, leaves, grit, and salt buildup do not wait for a quiet day. They hit during overnight shifts, early-morning arrivals, trailer moves, deliveries, handoffs, and extended storm cycles.

### Contractor variability during regional events

The same storm that affects one logistics site usually affects the surrounding region. Third-party crews are strained, routes get triaged, and lower-visibility paths can fall behind.

### Safety-sensitive zones near vehicles and equipment

Logistics sites include pedestrians, yard trucks, trailers, forklifts, spotters, security teams, visitors, and contractors. Outdoor maintenance has to be planned around traffic patterns, restricted areas, and approved routes.

### Proof gaps across high-activity sites

A route may be serviced, but leaders still need to know what was cleared, when it was treated, what weather triggered the mission, and why a route was paused or blocked.

Snowbotix gives logistics and terminal operators a controlled way to automate recurring outdoor routes while preserving oversight, route discipline, and service records.

## Section 4: The proof layer logistics teams can use

## Replace after-the-fact uncertainty with route-level service evidence.

Logistics and terminal leaders need to know more than whether someone was sent outside. They need to know which route was handled, when it was completed, what conditions were present, and whether anything blocked completion.

Snowbotix creates a documented operating record with:

- **Geocoded before-and-after imagery:** visual evidence tied to route, time, and location.
- **GPS path history:** route-level visibility into where the robot operated.
- **Mission status:** scheduled, active, paused, completed, interrupted, or escalated.
- **Area-serviced reporting:** mapped zones, route segments, square footage, or acreage covered.
- **Material application data:** liquid or granular de-icing records where the route requires treatment.
- **Weather and surface context:** site-level weather, temperature, precipitation, and accumulation signals.
- **Obstruction records:** blocked gate, fallen limb, unauthorized vehicle, pallet obstruction, debris, pedestrian cluster, or route interruption.
- **Supervisor notes:** human-in-the-loop review when routes require judgment, escalation, or facility follow-up.

For logistics leaders, this record can support operations reviews, vendor conversations, shift-change planning, facilities reporting, safety documentation, and budget evaluation.

The point is simple: completed work should not depend on memory, text messages, or a clipboard after the storm.

## Section 5: Controlled autonomy around people, vehicles, and active yards

## The right deployment starts with defined routes, not open-ended autonomy.

Logistics, port, and intermodal environments contain moving vehicles, pedestrians, trailers, containers, access controls, restricted zones, and variable traffic patterns. That makes deployment design as important as the robot itself.

Snowbotix uses a controlled operating model:

- **Site assessment and mapping** define route boundaries, operating windows, staging areas, charging locations, pile zones, and exclusion zones.
- **Geofenced missions** keep the robot on approved routes and out of areas that are not part of the deployment.
- **360-degree sensing** detects people, vehicles, equipment, and obstacles near the robot.
- **Six-foot safety radius** triggers an automatic halt when a person or object enters the defined safety zone.
- **Human-in-the-loop monitoring** provides remote oversight, mission support, and escalation.
- **Low-speed autonomous operation** supports controlled movement on unmanned routes.
- **Line-of-sight manual mode** supports repositioning or specific higher-complexity movements when an operator is present.
- **Supervisor alerts** turn obstructions into actionable site events instead of silent failures.

Snowbotix is strongest when the site team starts with clear operating rules: where the robot runs, when it runs, where it stops, who receives alerts, and how route exceptions are handled.

## Section 7: Buyer anxiety and how the page should answer it

## The concern is not whether automation sounds useful. The concern is whether it fits the yard.

### “Will it get in the way of trucks?”

Snowbotix should be deployed on approved outdoor maintenance routes with geofenced boundaries, defined service windows, site-specific exclusions, remote monitoring, and clear escalation protocols. It is not positioned as a robot moving freely through active truck traffic.

### “What happens if the route is blocked?”

The robot detects obstructions, pauses or stops based on its safety logic, and alerts the supervisor. The event can be documented with imagery, route status, and a follow-up note for the facility team.

### “Can it handle snow that changes from powder to slush to wet accumulation?”

Snowbotix supports different winter tools, including plows, V-blades, power brooms, and de-icing attachments. The right attachment strategy is defined during assessment based on route type, surface goals, accumulation patterns, and service frequency.

### “Will our team have to maintain another machine?”

Robot-as-a-Service can include hardware, software, maintenance, support, sensor calibration, attachments, and remote human-in-the-loop supervision. Deployment details should be structured around the buyer’s procurement and operating model.

### “Can this work across multiple sites?”

A strong rollout starts with one route or one facility. Once the operating model is validated, the same structure can expand to additional routes, facilities, seasons, and units.

### “How do we prove it worked?”

Snowbotix creates route-level service records with geocoded imagery, GPS history, mission status, runtime, material application logs, weather context, and obstruction events.

### “What if our site is too complex?”

Complexity is exactly why the process starts with route selection and site assessment. Snowbotix does not need to automate the entire property first. It needs one recurring route where value, control, and proof are clear.

## Section 9: Pilot-to-scale path

## Start with one high-friction route that can prove the operating model.

The best first deployment is narrow, observable, and operationally relevant.

**Strong pilot candidates:**

- Dock approach + warehouse entry + adjacent pedestrian route.
- Gatehouse + security checkpoint + driver check-in path.

- Yard office approach + maintenance shop route + fueling-area pedestrian path.
- Port office route + crew walkway + non-road terminal support corridor.
- Intermodal terminal building + employee walkway + gatehouse connector.

**Pilot process:**

1. **Select the route:** Choose a recurring outdoor route where weather, staffing, traffic, and proof gaps overlap.
2. **Map the operating environment:** Define geofences, route boundaries, exclusion zones, dock or gate interactions, staging, charging, and service windows.
3. **Set service goals:** Define what “service-ready” means for that route, such as cleared path, treated surface, swept corridor, documented completion, or obstruction notification.
4. **Run controlled missions:** Deploy Snowbotix on approved routes with remote supervision and site-team coordination.
5. **Review the operating record:** Evaluate route completion, timing, interruptions, safety stops, before-and-after imagery, material use, and route coverage.
6. **Decide expansion:** Add adjacent routes, additional shifts, more seasons, or additional sites once the pilot route proves operational fit.

## Section 11: FAQ

### Is Snowbotix meant to operate in active truck lanes?

Snowbotix should be deployed through a site-specific assessment. In logistics, port, and intermodal environments, the strongest starting point is usually a mapped support route, such as a dock-adjacent pedestrian path, gatehouse walkway, employee connector, terminal support route, or yard office approach. Active vehicle lanes require additional review, operating rules, and site approval.

### Can Snowbotix work around trailers, containers, and yard equipment?

Snowbotix uses 360-degree sensing, geofenced routes, low-speed autonomous operation, and human-in-the-loop monitoring. The deployment should define approved routes, exclusions, operating windows, and escalation rules around trailers, containers, yard equipment, and pedestrian traffic.

### What if a route is blocked by a trailer, pallet, debris, or snow pile?

Snowbotix can detect an obstruction, stop or pause according to its operating logic, and alert the remote supervisor. The event can be documented with imagery, location, mission status, and follow-up notes for the site team.

### Can Snowbotix support both snow removal and sweeping?

Yes. Snowbotix is a multi-utility platform that can support snow removal, de-icing, power brooming, sweeping, mowing, brush control, and route observation with the appropriate attachments and deployment plan.

### Does Snowbotix replace our snow contractor?

Snowbotix can operate as a direct site asset, a Robot-as-a-Service deployment, or part of a contractor-supported operating model. Many sites should start by using Snowbotix on defined recurring routes where staffing, timing, or verification gaps are most persistent.

### What route should we start with?

Start with the route that repeatedly creates friction: a dock approach, gatehouse path, employee shift-change walkway, terminal support route, yard office approach, or recurring sweeping corridor. The best first route is important enough to matter and defined enough to map.

### Does Snowbotix create records we can use internally?

Yes. Snowbotix creates proof-of-service records that can include geocoded imagery, GPS path history, mission status, runtime, material application logs, obstruction events, weather context, and supervisor notes.

### Can Snowbotix run at night?

Snowbotix is designed for supervised autonomous operation across approved routes and can support off-hour missions where site readiness, lighting, route mapping, connectivity, and operating protocols are appropriate.

### What infrastructure is needed?

Typical deployment planning includes route maps or aerial imagery, staging space, charging access, approved storage for de-icing materials where applicable, connectivity review, safety review, and a site owner who can coordinate facilities, operations, and safety input.

### How does the buying process work?

The recommended first step is a route assessment. Snowbotix reviews the route, operating constraints, seasonal maintenance needs, proof requirements, and deployment model. From there, the site can evaluate a pilot, RaaS deployment, lease, direct purchase, or contractor-supported model.
